Why Screening Matters

Breast cancer screening is a vital tool in the early detection of breast cancer. When found early, breast cancer is more likely to be smaller, less likely to have spread, and more likely to be treated successfully.

Regular screening can detect breast cancer before physical symptoms develop, when treatment is most effective.

Benefits of Regular Screening

  • Early detection of breast cancer
  • Better treatment outcomes
  • Peace of mind and regular monitoring
  • Professional medical assessment

Types of Screening

Mammogram

A mammogram is an X-ray picture of the breast that can detect tumors that cannot be felt. We use state-of-the-art 3D mammography for the most accurate screening.

Breast MRI

Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) uses magnetic fields to create detailed pictures of the breast tissue. Particularly useful for high-risk patients.

Clinical Breast Exam

A physical examination by a healthcare provider to check for any changes or abnormalities in the breast tissue.

Screening Guidelines

There are different guidelines for breast cancer screening based on your age, risk factors, and personal health history. We recommend following these general guidelines, but always consult with your healthcare provider about what's right for you.

Ages 40-44

Women have the option to start annual mammograms. Talk to your doctor about when to start based on your personal risk factors.

Ages 45-54

Women should get a mammogram every year. 3D mammography (tomosynthesis) is recommended when available.

Ages 55 and older

Women can switch to mammograms every 2 years, or continue annual screening, depending on personal preferences and risk factors.

High Risk Individuals

May need to start screening earlier (before age 40) and include breast MRI. This includes women with:

  • Known BRCA1 or BRCA2 gene mutations
  • First-degree relatives with these mutations
  • History of chest radiation between ages 10-30
  • Strong family history of breast cancer

What about breast self-exams?

Breast self-awareness is important. While formal breast self-exams are no longer universally recommended, being familiar with how your breasts normally look and feel can help you recognize changes that should be reported to your healthcare provider.
