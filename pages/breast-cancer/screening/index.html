Why Screening Matters

When breast cancer is detected early, the 5-year survival rate is 99%. Screening helps find breast cancer before there are symptoms, when it may be easier to treat. Regular screening is one of the most important things you can do for your breast health.

Screening Guidelines

Different organizations have slightly different recommendations for breast cancer screening. The following are general guidelines, but your healthcare provider may recommend a different screening schedule based on your personal risk factors.

Ages 20-39

  • Monthly breast self-examination
  • Clinical breast examination every 1-3 years
  • Risk assessment with healthcare provider
  • No routine mammograms unless high risk

Ages 40-49

  • Monthly breast self-examination
  • Annual clinical breast examination
  • Annual mammogram (recommended by some organizations, optional by others)
  • Risk assessment with healthcare provider

Ages 50-74

  • Monthly breast self-examination
  • Annual clinical breast examination
  • Annual or biennial mammogram (every 1-2 years)
  • Additional screening based on risk factors

Ages 75+

  • Continue screening as long as in good health
  • Discuss benefits and limitations with healthcare provider
  • Consider life expectancy and treatment preferences

Remember, these are general guidelines. Your healthcare provider may recommend a different screening schedule based on your personal risk factors. Talk to your doctor about what's right for you.

Types of Breast Cancer Screening

Mammogram

An X-ray picture of the breast. Mammograms can find tumors that are too small to feel. They can also find ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS), abnormal cells in the lining of a breast duct that may become invasive cancer.

How often: Annually or biennially (every 2 years) depending on age and risk factors.

Breast Ultrasound

Uses sound waves to create pictures of the inside of the breast. Often used as a follow-up test after an abnormal finding on a mammogram or for women with dense breast tissue.

How often: As needed based on other screening results or risk factors.

Breast MRI

Uses magnets and radio waves to take pictures of the breast. Usually reserved for women at high risk of breast cancer or to help diagnose breast problems.

How often: Annually for high-risk women, or as needed for diagnosis.

Breast Self-Examination

While breast self-exams are not a substitute for mammograms or clinical breast exams, they help you become familiar with how your breasts look and feel so you can report any changes to your healthcare provider.

1

Visual Inspection

Stand in front of a mirror with your arms at your sides. Look for any changes in breast size, shape, skin texture (dimpling, puckering, or bulging), or nipple appearance. Repeat with arms raised overhead and hands on hips, pressing firmly to flex chest muscles.

2

Lying Down Examination

Lie down and place a pillow under your right shoulder. Use your left hand to examine your right breast using the flat pads of your fingers. Move in a circular pattern from the outside to the center, covering the entire breast and armpit. Repeat on the left breast.

3

Shower Examination

Check your breasts while showering. Wet skin makes it easier to feel changes. Use the same circular motion as the lying down exam.

4

Nipple Check

Gently squeeze each nipple to check for discharge. Look for any changes in nipple appearance or direction.

Important: If you find a lump or notice any changes, don't panic. Most breast changes are not cancer, but you should contact your healthcare provider for an evaluation.

Screening for High-Risk Women

Women at high risk for breast cancer may need to start screening earlier and may benefit from additional screening tests. You may be at high risk if you have:

  • A known BRCA1 or BRCA2 gene mutation
  • A first-degree relative (parent, sibling, or child) with a BRCA mutation
  • A personal history of breast cancer, DCIS, LCIS, or precancerous breast changes
  • A strong family history of breast or ovarian cancer
  • Had radiation therapy to the chest between ages 10-30
  • Certain genetic syndromes like Li-Fraumeni or Cowden syndrome

If you are at high risk, your healthcare provider may recommend:

  • Starting mammograms earlier (often at age 30)
  • Having breast MRIs in addition to mammograms
  • More frequent clinical breast exams
  • Genetic counseling and possible testing
  • Medications or surgery to reduce risk

What to Expect During Screening

Preparing for a Mammogram

  • Schedule your mammogram for the week after your period when your breasts are less tender
  • Don't wear deodorant, perfume, lotion, or powder under your arms or on your breasts on the day of the exam
  • Wear a two-piece outfit so you only need to remove your top
  • Bring previous mammogram records if you're going to a new facility

During the Mammogram

During a mammogram, your breast is placed on a flat support plate and compressed with a parallel plate. Compression is necessary to spread the breast tissue for better images and to reduce radiation exposure. You'll be asked to hold your breath for a few seconds while the X-ray is taken. The entire procedure takes about 20 minutes.

After the Mammogram

You can resume normal activities immediately after your mammogram. You will usually receive results within a few days to a week. If there are any concerning findings, you may need additional imaging or a biopsy.

Frequently Asked Questions

Mammograms can be uncomfortable due to the compression of the breast tissue, but the discomfort is brief. Some women report more discomfort if they have the exam just before or during their period when breasts may be more sensitive. Taking an over-the-counter pain reliever about an hour before the exam may help.

The dose of radiation from a mammogram is very low. For most women, the benefits of mammography in detecting cancer early outweigh the small risks from radiation exposure. Modern mammography equipment uses the lowest radiation dose possible while still producing quality images.

Dense breasts have more glandular and fibrous tissue than fatty tissue. Dense breast tissue can make it harder to see cancer on a mammogram. If you have dense breasts, your doctor may recommend additional screening tests such as ultrasound or MRI. Talk to your healthcare provider about your breast density and whether additional screening tests would be beneficial.

There is no specific age at which mammograms should stop. The decision should be based on your overall health, life expectancy, and personal preferences. Many guidelines suggest continuing mammograms as long as a woman is in good health and would be a candidate for breast cancer treatment. Talk with your healthcare provider about what's right for you.
