Treatment Planning

Your Treatment Plan

Your treatment plan will be based on multiple factors, including:

  • The type and stage of breast cancer
  • Whether the cancer cells have hormone receptors or HER2 protein
  • Your overall health and personal preferences
  • Whether you have gone through menopause
  • The genomic markers of your cancer (gene activity patterns)

Most people with breast cancer undergo surgery to remove the cancer, followed by other treatments to reduce the risk of recurrence.

Surgery

Surgery is usually the first line of attack against breast cancer. The type of surgery recommended will depend on various factors, including the size and stage of the cancer, the type of breast cancer, and your personal preferences.

Lumpectomy (Breast-Conserving Surgery)

This surgery removes only the tumor and a small amount of surrounding normal tissue. Lumpectomy is typically followed by radiation therapy to reduce the risk of recurrence. Most women with early-stage breast cancer are candidates for lumpectomy.

Mastectomy

Mastectomy involves removing all breast tissue. There are several types of mastectomy:

  • Total (Simple) Mastectomy: Removes the entire breast but not the lymph nodes or muscle tissue.
  • Modified Radical Mastectomy: Removes the entire breast and some lymph nodes.
  • Skin-Sparing Mastectomy: Preserves the skin over the breast for reconstruction.
  • Nipple-Sparing Mastectomy: Preserves the skin, nipple, and areola for reconstruction.

Lymph Node Surgery

To determine if cancer has spread to lymph nodes, one of these procedures may be performed:

  • Sentinel Lymph Node Biopsy: Identifies and removes the first lymph node(s) most likely to be affected.
  • Axillary Lymph Node Dissection: Removes multiple lymph nodes in the armpit if cancer is found in the sentinel nodes.

Types of Treatment

In addition to surgery, your treatment plan may include one or more of the following therapies:

Chemotherapy

Uses drugs to kill cancer cells throughout the body. May be given before surgery (neoadjuvant) or after surgery (adjuvant).

Radiation Therapy

Uses high-energy beams to kill cancer cells in a specific area. Often given after lumpectomy to reduce the risk of recurrence.

Hormone Therapy

Blocks hormone receptors or lowers hormone levels to prevent cancer growth. Used for hormone receptor-positive cancers.

Targeted Therapy

Attacks specific abnormalities within cancer cells. Includes HER2-targeted drugs for HER2-positive cancers.

Immunotherapy

Helps the immune system recognize and attack cancer cells. Being explored in clinical trials for breast cancer.

Clinical Trials

Participating in Research

Clinical trials are research studies that test new treatments to see if they are safe and effective. Participating in a clinical trial may give you access to treatments that are not otherwise available.

Find a Clinical Trial

Speak with your doctor about clinical trials that might be right for you, or search the National Cancer Institute's database at cancer.gov/clinicaltrials

Understand the Trial

Learn about the purpose of the trial, what treatments are being studied, and what tests and procedures are involved.

Assess Eligibility

Each trial has specific criteria for who can participate, based on factors such as cancer type, stage, previous treatments, and overall health.

Weigh Benefits and Risks

Consider the potential benefits (access to new treatments, close monitoring) and risks (unknown side effects, possibly ineffective treatment).

Managing Side Effects

Breast cancer treatments can cause side effects. Many side effects are temporary and can be managed with medications or lifestyle changes.

Physical Side Effects

  • Fatigue
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Hair loss
  • Lymphedema (swelling)
  • Pain or numbness

Emotional Side Effects

  • Anxiety
  • Depression
  • Fear of recurrence
  • Body image concerns
  • Stress

Long-Term Effects

  • Fertility issues
  • Menopausal symptoms
  • Heart health concerns
  • Cognitive changes
  • Bone health issues

Always discuss any side effects with your healthcare team. Many resources are available to help manage side effects and improve your quality of life during and after treatment.

Getting Quality Care

Ensuring the Best Care

Receiving high-quality care is essential for the best possible outcome. Here are some tips for ensuring you receive quality care:

  • Seek care at a breast cancer center or from providers who specialize in breast cancer
  • Ask about the treatment team's experience with your specific type of breast cancer
  • Get a second opinion, especially for major treatment decisions
  • Bring a friend or family member to appointments to help remember information
  • Keep records of all your test results, treatments, and side effects
  • Ask about support services available, including financial counseling
  • Consider joining a support group to connect with others going through similar experiences

Remember that you are your own best advocate. Don't hesitate to ask questions and express your concerns.
