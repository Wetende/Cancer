Overview

Paget's disease of the breast is a rare type of breast cancer that starts in the breast ducts and spreads to the skin of the nipple and areola. It's often associated with an underlying breast cancer, either ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS) or invasive breast cancer.

Signs and Symptoms

Early symptoms often resemble benign skin conditions, which can delay diagnosis. Common symptoms include:

  • Scaly, red, or crusty skin on the nipple and areola
  • Itching or burning sensation
  • Nipple flattening or inversion
  • Yellow or bloody discharge
  • Thickened skin on the breast
  • A lump in the affected breast
  • Symptoms usually affecting only one breast

Important Note

These symptoms can be mistaken for eczema or dermatitis. If skin changes on your breast persist for more than a few weeks, consult a healthcare provider.

Diagnosis

Diagnosing Paget's disease typically involves several steps:

  • Physical examination of the breast
  • Skin biopsy of the affected area
  • Mammogram to check for underlying breast cancer
  • Breast MRI for detailed imaging
  • Additional imaging tests if needed

Treatment Options

Treatment depends on whether there's underlying breast cancer and its extent. Options include:

Surgery

Either breast-conserving surgery or mastectomy, depending on the extent of the disease.

Radiation

Often recommended after breast-conserving surgery to reduce recurrence risk.

Chemotherapy

May be needed if there's invasive cancer or high risk of recurrence.

Hormone Therapy

Used for hormone receptor-positive cancers to prevent recurrence.

Prognosis

The prognosis for Paget's disease depends on several factors:

  • Presence and stage of underlying breast cancer
  • Whether the cancer has spread to lymph nodes
  • Hormone receptor status
  • Response to treatment
  • Overall health and age
