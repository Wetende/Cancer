Types of Invasive Breast Cancer

Invasive Ductal Carcinoma (IDC)

The most common type of invasive breast cancer, accounting for about 80% of all cases. It begins in the milk ducts and spreads into surrounding breast tissue.

Invasive Lobular Carcinoma (ILC)

The second most common type, starting in the milk-producing lobules. It can be harder to detect on mammograms and may appear in multiple areas of the breast.

Signs and Symptoms

Common Symptoms

  • A new lump or mass in the breast
  • Changes in breast size or shape
  • Skin changes (dimpling, redness, or thickening)
  • Nipple changes or discharge
  • Breast pain or tenderness
  • Swelling in the armpit area

Diagnosis

Diagnostic Process

Diagnosis typically involves multiple steps and may include:

  • Physical examination
  • Mammogram and/or breast ultrasound
  • Breast MRI
  • Biopsy to confirm cancer presence
  • Additional imaging to check for spread

Treatment Options

Treatment plans are personalized based on various factors including cancer stage, type, and individual patient characteristics.

Surgery

Options include lumpectomy (breast-conserving surgery) or mastectomy (removal of the entire breast).

Radiation Therapy

Often used after surgery to destroy any remaining cancer cells and reduce the risk of recurrence.

Chemotherapy

May be given before surgery (neoadjuvant) or after surgery (adjuvant) to kill cancer cells throughout the body.

Hormone Therapy

For hormone receptor-positive cancers, helps prevent cancer growth stimulated by hormones.

Prognosis

Survival Rates and Outlook

Prognosis depends on several factors including:

  • Stage at diagnosis
  • Tumor grade and type
  • Hormone receptor status
  • HER2 status
  • Age and overall health

With early detection and advancing treatment options, survival rates continue to improve. The 5-year survival rate for localized invasive breast cancer is approximately 99%.
