Overview

Inflammatory breast cancer (IBC) is a rare and aggressive form of breast cancer that accounts for 1-5% of all breast cancer cases. Unlike other breast cancers, IBC typically doesn't present as a lump and can be challenging to diagnose.

Important Notice

IBC is a rapidly progressing disease that requires immediate medical attention. If you notice sudden changes in your breast, especially redness and swelling, consult a healthcare provider right away.

Signs and Symptoms

IBC symptoms typically develop quickly, over a period of weeks or months. Common symptoms include:

  • Breast swelling, often sudden and significant
  • Redness affecting at least 1/3 of the breast
  • Skin that appears pink, reddish purple, or bruised
  • Skin that feels warm to the touch
  • Skin changes that look like an orange peel (peau d'orange)
  • Inverted or flattened nipple
  • Breast tenderness or pain
  • Swollen lymph nodes under the arm or near the collarbone

Diagnosis

Diagnosing IBC can be challenging because it presents differently than other breast cancers. The diagnostic process typically includes:

  • Clinical breast examination
  • Diagnostic mammogram and breast ultrasound
  • Breast MRI
  • Skin punch biopsy
  • PET scan or CT scan to check for spread

Why Quick Diagnosis Matters

Because IBC progresses rapidly, prompt diagnosis and treatment are crucial for the best possible outcomes. If you experience symptoms, don't wait to seek medical attention.

Treatment Options

Treatment for IBC typically follows a multi-modal approach:

Chemotherapy

Usually the first line of treatment (neoadjuvant chemotherapy) to reduce the cancer before other treatments.

Surgery

Modified radical mastectomy is typically performed after chemotherapy has reduced the cancer.

Radiation

Post-surgery radiation therapy helps destroy any remaining cancer cells and reduce recurrence risk.

Targeted Therapy

May be used if the cancer is HER2-positive or has other specific characteristics.

Prognosis

The prognosis for IBC depends on several factors:

  • How quickly the cancer is diagnosed and treated
  • Response to initial chemotherapy
  • Stage at diagnosis
  • Hormone receptor and HER2 status
  • Overall health and age
