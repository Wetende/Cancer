Breast Cancer Diagnosis

Understanding a breast cancer diagnosis can be overwhelming. Learn about the diagnostic process, types of biopsies, how to interpret pathology reports, and important questions to ask your healthcare team.

Initial Evaluation

The diagnostic process typically begins when a person or their healthcare provider notices a breast change or abnormality. Initial evaluation may include:

  • Clinical breast examination by a healthcare provider
  • Review of personal and family medical history
  • Discussion of symptoms and changes you've noticed

If there are concerns about possible breast cancer, your healthcare provider will recommend diagnostic imaging tests.

Diagnostic Imaging

Several imaging tests may be used to evaluate breast changes and help determine if a biopsy is needed:

Diagnostic Mammogram

A more detailed X-ray examination than a screening mammogram. It focuses on areas of concern and may involve additional views of the breast. Diagnostic mammograms are used when there's a breast symptom or an abnormal finding on a screening mammogram.

Breast Ultrasound

Uses sound waves to create images of breast tissue. Particularly useful for distinguishing between fluid-filled cysts and solid masses, and for evaluating lumps in women under 30 or pregnant women (who shouldn't be exposed to X-rays).

Breast MRI

Uses magnetic fields and radio waves to create detailed images of breast tissue. May be used when more information is needed after other imaging tests, or to screen high-risk women. Contrast dye is usually injected to highlight abnormal areas.

Other Imaging

Additional tests like 3D mammography (tomosynthesis), breast-specific gamma imaging, or molecular breast imaging may be used in some cases.

If imaging tests suggest a suspicious area that might be cancer, the next step is usually a biopsy to remove some cells or tissue for examination under a microscope.

Breast Biopsies

A biopsy is the only definitive way to diagnose breast cancer. During a biopsy, a small amount of tissue or cells is removed from the suspicious area and examined under a microscope by a pathologist. There are several types of breast biopsies:

Fine Needle Aspiration (FNA)

A thin needle is used to withdraw a small sample of cells from a breast lump. This is the least invasive type of biopsy but doesn't always provide enough tissue for a complete diagnosis.

  • Procedure Time: About 5-10 minutes
  • Recovery: Minimal; can return to normal activities immediately
  • Scarring: None to minimal

Core Needle Biopsy

A larger needle is used to remove small cylinders (cores) of tissue from the abnormal area. This is the most common type of biopsy and is usually guided by ultrasound, stereotactic (mammography), or MRI.

  • Procedure Time: About 20-30 minutes
  • Recovery: Quick; usually 24 hours
  • Scarring: Minimal

Surgical Biopsy

A surgeon removes all or part of the abnormal area through an incision in the breast. There are two types:

  • Excisional: Removes the entire abnormal area
  • Incisional: Removes only part of the abnormal area
  • Procedure Time: About 30-60 minutes
  • Recovery: Several days
  • Scarring: More noticeable than needle biopsies

Vacuum-Assisted Biopsy

Uses a vacuum-powered instrument to collect multiple tissue samples with a single insertion. Often used for microcalcifications or hard-to-reach areas.

  • Procedure Time: About 30-45 minutes
  • Recovery: Usually 24-48 hours
  • Scarring: Minimal

Note: Most biopsies are done as outpatient procedures with local anesthesia. Your healthcare team will recommend the most appropriate type of biopsy based on the location and characteristics of the abnormal area.

Understanding Pathology Reports

After a biopsy, the tissue sample is sent to a laboratory where a pathologist examines it under a microscope. The pathology report provides crucial information about the breast cells and helps determine treatment options.

Key elements of a breast cancer pathology report include:

Pathology Element What It Means
Type of Breast Cancer The most common types are ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS), invasive ductal carcinoma, and invasive lobular carcinoma.
Tumor Grade Indicates how abnormal the cancer cells look and how quickly they are likely to grow and spread. Graded 1 (low-grade, slow growing) to 3 (high-grade, fast growing).
Tumor Size The size of the tumor in centimeters.
Hormone Receptor Status Whether the cancer cells have receptors for estrogen (ER) and progesterone (PR). Hormone receptor-positive cancers can be treated with hormone therapy.
HER2 Status Whether the cancer cells make too much of a protein called HER2. HER2-positive cancers can be treated with targeted therapies.
Lymph Node Status Whether cancer cells were found in the lymph nodes, which indicates if the cancer has spread beyond the breast.
Margins Whether the edges of the removed tissue are free of cancer cells. "Clear" or "negative" margins mean no cancer cells were found at the edge.
Lymphovascular Invasion Whether cancer cells were found in blood vessels or lymphatic vessels, which may indicate a higher risk of spread.

Some patients may also have genomic testing (such as Oncotype DX, MammaPrint, or PAM50) to provide additional information about the risk of recurrence and the potential benefit of chemotherapy.

Cancer Staging

Staging describes the extent of cancer in the body and helps guide treatment decisions. The most common staging system for breast cancer is the TNM system:

  • T (Tumor): Size and extent of the main tumor
  • N (Nodes): Whether cancer has spread to nearby lymph nodes
  • M (Metastasis): Whether cancer has spread to distant parts of the body

Based on TNM factors, breast cancer is assigned a stage from 0 to IV:

  • Stage 0: Non-invasive cancer (DCIS)
  • Stage I: Small, invasive cancer that has not spread
  • Stage II: Cancer is larger or has spread to few nearby lymph nodes
  • Stage III: Cancer has spread to many lymph nodes or nearby tissues
  • Stage IV: Cancer has spread to distant organs (metastatic)

The American Joint Committee on Cancer (AJCC) now also includes biomarkers (hormone receptor and HER2 status) and tumor grade in its staging system, as these factors significantly influence prognosis and treatment options.

Important Questions to Ask

After a breast cancer diagnosis, it's important to gather information to understand your condition and make informed treatment decisions. Here are some questions to ask your healthcare team:

About Your Diagnosis

  • What type of breast cancer do I have?
  • What is the stage and grade of my cancer?
  • Are my hormone receptors positive or negative?
  • Is my cancer HER2-positive or negative?
  • Has the cancer spread to my lymph nodes or other parts of my body?
  • What does my pathology report tell you about my prognosis?

About Treatment Options

  • What are my treatment options?
  • Which treatments do you recommend and why?
  • What are the benefits and risks of each treatment?
  • How will each treatment affect my daily life?
  • Are there clinical trials I should consider?
  • How quickly do I need to make a decision about treatment?

About Your Healthcare Team

  • Who will coordinate my care?
  • Which specialists will I see?
  • How many patients with my type of cancer have you treated?
  • How do I contact you or your team if I have questions?
  • What support resources are available to me?
  • Should I get a second opinion?

Getting a Second Opinion

Seeking a second opinion is a common and often recommended step after a breast cancer diagnosis. A second opinion can:

  • Confirm your diagnosis
  • Provide additional treatment options
  • Give you peace of mind about your treatment plan
  • Help you feel more confident in your healthcare team

Most doctors understand the value of second opinions and will not be offended if you seek one. Many insurance plans cover second opinions, but check with your insurer before making appointments.

When seeking a second opinion:

  • Ask your doctor to recommend another specialist or look for specialists at National Cancer Institute-designated cancer centers
  • Gather all relevant medical records, including imaging studies and pathology reports
  • Prepare a list of questions, especially about areas where you're uncertain
  • Consider bringing a family member or friend to help take notes

Remember that the goal is to make informed decisions about your care. If the second opinion differs significantly from the first, you might consider getting a third opinion or asking the two doctors to discuss your case.
