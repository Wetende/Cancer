Breast Cancer Risk Factors

Understanding your risk factors for breast cancer can help you make informed decisions about screening and lifestyle choices. Learn about both modifiable and non-modifiable risk factors, genetic testing, and ways to reduce your risk.

Understanding Breast Cancer Risk

A risk factor is anything that increases your chances of developing breast cancer. Having one or more risk factors doesn't mean you'll definitely get breast cancer. Similarly, having no risk factors doesn't mean you won't get breast cancer.

Important to Know

  • Most women who develop breast cancer have no known risk factors other than being female and getting older
  • Most women with one or more risk factors will never develop breast cancer
  • Some risk factors can be changed (modifiable) while others cannot (non-modifiable)

Established Risk Factors

Non-Modifiable Risk Factors

Age

The risk of breast cancer increases with age. Most breast cancers are diagnosed after age 50.

Gender

Being female is the main risk factor for breast cancer, though men can also develop the disease.

Family History

Having a first-degree relative (mother, sister, daughter) with breast cancer approximately doubles the risk.

Genetic Mutations

Inherited mutations in certain genes (BRCA1, BRCA2) significantly increase breast cancer risk.

Personal History

A personal history of breast cancer or certain non-cancerous breast diseases increases risk.

Dense Breast Tissue

Women with dense breasts have a higher risk of breast cancer than women with less dense breast tissue.

Modifiable Risk Factors

Physical Activity

Regular physical activity can help lower breast cancer risk.

Body Weight

Being overweight or obese after menopause increases breast cancer risk.

Alcohol Consumption

Drinking alcohol increases risk. The more alcohol consumed, the greater the risk.

Smoking

Smoking is linked to a higher risk of breast cancer, particularly in premenopausal women.

Hormone Therapy

Combined hormone therapy after menopause can increase breast cancer risk.

Genetic Testing and Counseling

Who Should Consider Genetic Testing?

  • People with a family history of breast cancer, especially at a young age
  • Those with multiple family members affected by breast or ovarian cancer
  • People of Ashkenazi Jewish descent
  • Individuals with male breast cancer in their family
  • Those with a personal history of breast cancer at a young age

The Genetic Testing Process

  1. Initial consultation with a genetic counselor
  2. Review of personal and family medical history
  3. Risk assessment and discussion of testing options
  4. Testing (if appropriate)
  5. Results interpretation and management plan

Genetic counseling is available at several locations in Bellevue. Contact us for referrals to certified genetic counselors.

Risk Reduction Strategies

Lifestyle Changes

  • Maintain a healthy weight
  • Exercise regularly
  • Limit alcohol consumption
  • Quit smoking
  • Eat a healthy diet

Medical Options

  • Regular screening mammograms
  • Clinical breast exams
  • Risk-reducing medications (for high-risk individuals)
  • Preventive surgery (for very high-risk individuals)

Risk Assessment Tools

Several tools are available to help estimate your breast cancer risk. These tools consider various factors including:

  • Age
  • Family history
  • Reproductive history
  • Personal medical history
  • Breast density

Speak with your healthcare provider about which risk assessment tool is most appropriate for you.
